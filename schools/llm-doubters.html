LLM-paradigm doubters

Whatever LLMs are, they're not the architecture that gets to AGI. Word models aren't world models. Scaling a thing that can't do X will never give you X, no matter how much compute you spend.

Different from empiricists. Empiricists say the evidence doesn't yet show imminent AGI. LLM-paradigm doubters say the architecture itself is wrong — scaling is measuring a plateau, not climbing toward a ceiling.

The argument

The cleanest recent statement is Latent Space's "Experts Have World Models. LLMs Have Word Models." The claim: real reasoning requires an internal model of how things work, not just statistics over how people describe them. LLMs do well on questions whose answers humans have written down, and poorly on questions that require simulating the thing itself. Yann LeCun's public writing is the senior-researcher version of the same argument.

The infrastructure angle comes from "Expensively Quadratic": if LLM agents have quadratic cost curves and sub-linear capability gains on hard reasoning, the economics break before the capability arrives. "Cracking the Claw" (CTO Lunch NYC) is the operator-side skepticism: what the agents actually fail at, in production.

Counter-position: Empiricists who think the current trajectory still matters (e.g., Honnibal's reasoning-RL argument); Extrapolators who think mechanism doesn't matter if the curves are straight.
