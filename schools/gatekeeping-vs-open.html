Gatekeeping vs. open

Should dangerous capabilities be held back? The labs say yes — their safety argument requires controlled release. But when public models replicate the feats in a weekend, the gatekeeping argument starts to look less like safety and more like moat maintenance.

The load-bearing evidence. Vidoc Security Lab reproduced Anthropic's Mythos vulnerability-finding claims using public models for under $30 per target. If the "dangerous" capability is replicable with open-source, the moat is operations, not model access.

The argument

The centerpiece is Vidoc Security Lab's "We Reproduced Anthropic's Mythos Findings With Public Models". Using GPT-5.4, Claude Opus 4.6, and the opencode agent, Vidoc ran five targets (FreeBSD, OpenBSD, Botan, FFmpeg, wolfSSL). Both models hit exact reproductions on FreeBSD and Botan (3/3). Claude 4.6 solo nailed OpenBSD (3/3, where GPT failed). Partial wins on FFmpeg and wolfSSL. Total cost under $30 per file. The conclusion: the moat isn't model access, it's validation, prioritization, and operationalization.

The policy implication is that "we can't release this, it's too dangerous" deserves far more scrutiny as a claim. The constitutional-governance framing (see Constitutional) and the critiques of it (see Constitutionalism critiques) intersect here — both apply pressure on how labs get to define "dangerous."
