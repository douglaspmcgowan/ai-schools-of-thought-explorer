No product

The capability line is rising continuously. Whatever sits below it stops being a defensible product because customers can rebuild cheaply. Pricing pivots — "outcome-based," "add AI" — are treating the symptom, not the cause.

Why this cuts deep. Most SaaS businesses assume an operating margin supported by switching costs. If the capability line eats those switching costs, the margin goes with them.

The argument

Sidu Ponnappa's "There Is No Product" is this school's defining essay. The thesis: the AI capability line rises continuously; whatever sits below it stops being a defensible product because customers can rebuild it cheaply with an LLM and a weekend. The pricing pivots people are trying — "outcome-based," "add AI" — treat symptoms, not the cause. Real products persist only where building remains genuinely hard: compilers, SOTA models, specialized engines. The essay is hostile to most current SaaS economics, and it's well-argued enough to be worth taking seriously even if you disagree.

Ben Evans's "How will OpenAI compete?" is the adjacent piece for why even the labs face the same problem: as capability becomes a commodity, the question isn't whose model is best, it's whose distribution is deepest. Fintech Brainfood's "The Claw Economy" extends the frame into financial services. "Mercenary vs. Missionary Startups" reads usefully as a framing for which startups survive.

Direct counter-position: SaaS survives. Partial counter: Not a bubble, which accepts the capability line but argues reasoning-RL keeps enough differentiation alive.
