Compound engineering

Stack agents across a long horizon: one plans, one builds, one reviews, one ships. The output of each feeds the next. Specialization happens at the stage boundary, not inside a single agent's head.

The name comes from Every. Kieran Klaassen is the figure most associated with the practice — his work has 14k GitHub stars and drove Every's own workflow. The pattern is closer to a CI/CD pipeline than to a multi-agent swarm.

The argument

Every's Compound Engineering Camp essay (2026-04-16) names the practice and profiles Klaassen's work. The claim is empirical: sequential, specialized agents — each good at one stage — beat generalist agents asked to do the whole job. Planning agents plan. Review agents review. They're bad at each other's jobs, and that's the point. Related Every pieces show it operating: What I Learned Onboarding Our AI Project Manager (2026-03-31) and Seven Things I've Learned Getting Companies to Use AI (2026-03-30).

This school is where spec-driven, folder-as-agent, and org design converge operationally: the specs are artifacts passed between stages, each stage is a folder, and the company chart mirrors the pipeline. Every's own How We Run a 25-Person Company on Four AI Agents (2026-04-09) is the organizational writeup. Tom Tunguz's "9 Observations on Building with AI Agents" is the investor-side read of the pattern.
