SaaS survives

Good SaaS was never just "a form over a DB." Workflow, data, trust, and distribution still matter — and still compound. The capability line rising threatens the bad SaaS, not the good SaaS.

The operational corrective. Companies like Linear keep growing in the AI era not despite the capability curve but because they've been compounding the non-model parts of the stack all along.

The argument

Every's If SaaS Is Dead, Linear Didn't Get the Memo (2026-04-01) is the defining essay. The argument: the "SaaS is dead" takes conflate generic SaaS (thin wrappers around CRUD) with SaaS-as-practice (compounding advantages in workflow, data, distribution, trust). Linear — and Figma, Notion, Ramp — keep growing because what they sell is the compounded advantage, not the raw feature set. An LLM + a weekend can rebuild the feature set. It cannot rebuild the trust, the integrations, or the ten years of product-decision context.

Tom Tunguz's "9 Observations on Building with AI Agents" is the investor-side read of which SaaS companies are adapting well: the ones treating AI as a feature in a larger value prop, not the value prop itself. "Strategic Choices: When Both Options Are Good" (A Smart Bear) is the meta-strategy companion — even durable SaaS has to make active choices in this era.

Sequoia's "The Opening, Midgame, and Endgame in Startups" is the venture-side version: structural advantages matter more as markets mature, and the AI disruption is a mid-game event for most categories, not a reset.

Direct counter-position: No product.
