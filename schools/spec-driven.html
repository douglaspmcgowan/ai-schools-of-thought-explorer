Spec-driven

Write the spec; the agent writes the code. The artifact the human actually produces and reviews is the specification — tests, acceptance criteria, edge-case lists — not the implementation. The agent's job is to satisfy the spec.

What it's reacting to. Autonomous agents regularly get to 80% on a task and can't finish. Spec-driven folks say: that's because you under-specified. Pay the planning tax up front, reap the determinism later.

The argument

Addy Osmani is this school's most prolific voice. His "80% Problem in Agentic Coding" diagnoses the failure mode: agents predictably stall on non-trivial tasks, not because the model is weak but because the task definition is. His companion essay "Agentic Engineering" names the discipline — spec-first, acceptance-criteria-driven, with the agent as executor.

The tactical half lives in official prompting guides that, read closely, are themselves endorsements of this school: the Codex Prompting Guide is basically a style manual for specs. And "Your LLM Doesn't Write Correct Code. It Writes Plausible Code" is the short version of why you need explicit correctness criteria rather than trusting the output looks right.

Operationally, the spec-driven school overlaps heavily with compound engineering (where specs become a stage in the pipeline) and with the folder-as-agent pattern (where each agent's folder is partly its spec). What distinguishes spec-driven as its own school is the commitment to specs as the human's primary deliverable.
