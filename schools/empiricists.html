Empiricists

Look at what models can actually do today. ARC-AGI scores, math benchmarks, reasoning transfer. The gap between the marketing and the measurements is large, and the marketing is what's being used to set policy and valuations.

Not the same as doomers. Empiricists aren't anti-AI. They argue that near-term timelines are being sold on extrapolations that the actual measurements don't yet support.

The argument

The defining recent essay is dlants, "Why I don't think AGI is imminent". The thesis: LLMs lack innate cognitive primitives — object permanence, number sense, causal reasoning, spatial navigation — that evolution spent hundreds of millions of years selecting for. Text and video observation are not substitutes for embodied learning. The author cites ARC-AGI evidence (pure LLMs at 0%, frontier models at single digits, high scores requiring scaffolding) and the 2025 AAAI survey finding that 76% of researchers consider "scaling current approaches" unlikely to reach AGI.

The most rigorous counterpoint is Matt Honnibal, "Why I don't think AI is a bubble". Honnibal is close to the empiricist camp in method — mechanism-driven, skeptical of pitch decks — but argues that reasoning-RL changes the scaling story in a way the ARC-AGI bears haven't absorbed. The two essays together define the live debate inside the empiricist tendency.

IEEE Spectrum's "How Far Can AI Go in Solving Math Mysteries" is the benchmark-journalism companion piece. For the macro-economic empiricist move — asking where the productivity actually shows up — see "You see tech and AI everywhere, but not in the productivity statistics".

Counter-positions: Extrapolators, who argue the trend lines are load-bearing even absent a mechanism; LLM-paradigm doubters, who go further and argue the architecture is wrong.
